The Mechanics
Behind EquiCurve

A deep dive into the mathematical models, economic principles, and strategic frameworks that power dynamic token crowdfunding simulations

The Core Demand Model

At the heart of EquiCurve lies a multiplicative demand function that captures the complex interactions between price, effort, and market dynamics in token crowdfunding campaigns.

D(t) = α × E(t)β × P(t)-γ × ε(t)
where t represents time (day) in the campaign

Component Breakdown

  • α (Alpha): Base demand level representing the total addressable market size. Higher values indicate larger potential buyer pools.
  • E(t): Entrepreneur effort at time t, measured on a 0-10 scale. Represents marketing, community engagement, and project development intensity.
  • β (Beta): Effort elasticity (typically 0.3-0.7). Determines how responsive demand is to changes in effort. Higher values mean effort has more impact.
  • P(t): Token price at time t in USD. The price point at which tokens are offered to backers.
  • γ (Gamma): Price elasticity (typically 0.8-1.5). Measures price sensitivity. Values > 1 indicate elastic demand (price changes significantly affect demand).
  • ε(t): Random market noise following a log-normal distribution. Captures unpredictable market fluctuations and external factors.

Why This Model?

  • Multiplicative Structure: Reflects real-world interactions where effort and price effects compound rather than simply add.
  • Log-Linear Properties: Enables easy transformation to linear regression for parameter estimation from historical data.
  • Empirical Foundation: Based on established economic literature on crowdfunding (Belleflamme et al., 2014) and Web3 tokenomics research.
  • Flexibility: Parameters can be calibrated to match different market segments, from early-stage startups to established Web3 projects.
  • Realistic Dynamics: The negative price exponent ensures higher prices reduce demand, while positive effort exponent rewards active marketing.

Pricing Strategies

EquiCurve implements three distinct pricing strategies, each with unique characteristics and optimal use cases:

1

Fixed Price Strategy

Simple, predictable, and transparent

How it works:

The token price remains constant throughout the campaign: P(t) = P₀ (initial price). Effort is also held constant at a predetermined level (typically 5 on a 0-10 scale).

Advantages

  • ✓ Predictable for backers
  • ✓ Simple to implement
  • ✓ Low information requirements
  • ✓ Reduces price speculation

Disadvantages

  • ✗ Cannot adapt to market conditions
  • ✗ May leave money on the table
  • ✗ Risk of underselling if demand is high
  • ✗ No mechanism to incentivize early backers
2

Dynamic Pricing Strategy

Adaptive, responsive, and market-driven

How it works:

Price adjusts based on campaign progress relative to time: P(t) = P₀ × (1 + adjustment), where adjustment = (progress_ratio - time_ratio) × rate. If the campaign is ahead of schedule, prices increase. If behind, prices decrease to stimulate demand. Effort is dynamically allocated, front-loading when behind target and conserving when ahead.

Advantages

  • ✓ Maximizes revenue potential
  • ✓ Self-correcting mechanism
  • ✓ Adapts to real-time market feedback
  • ✓ Optimizes effort allocation

Disadvantages

  • ✗ Complex to communicate
  • ✗ May create price speculation
  • ✗ Requires real-time monitoring
  • ✗ Risk of price manipulation
3

Bonding Curve Strategy

Automated market maker (AMM) based pricing

How it works:

Price increases automatically as more tokens are sold: P(t) = P₀ × (1 + curve_param × tokens_sold / target_tokens). This creates an automated market where early buyers get better prices, incentivizing early participation. Effort scales with price to maintain demand as prices rise.

Advantages

  • ✓ Rewards early backers
  • ✓ Automated price discovery
  • ✓ Creates liquidity mechanism
  • ✓ Aligns with Web3 principles

Disadvantages

  • ✗ Price volatility risk
  • ✗ Complex for non-crypto users
  • ✗ May discourage late participants
  • ✗ Requires careful parameter tuning

The Simulation Process

1

Initialization

Set campaign parameters: target amount, duration (days), initial price, and model parameters (α, β, γ). Choose a pricing strategy and initialize the simulation state.

2

Daily Iteration

For each day t in the campaign:

  • Calculate current price P(t) using selected strategy
  • Determine effort level E(t) based on strategy and campaign progress
  • Generate random market noise ε(t) from log-normal distribution
  • Compute demand D(t) using the core equation
  • Calculate daily revenue: revenue(t) = D(t) × P(t)
  • Update cumulative raised amount
  • Store all metrics in history

3

Cost Calculation

Calculate total campaign costs using the effort cost function: Cost(E) = c₁ × E + c₂ × E². This quadratic function captures increasing marginal costs of effort (diminishing returns). Total costs = Σ Cost(E(t)) over all days.

4

Results Analysis

Evaluate campaign success: compare total raised against target. Calculate key metrics:

  • Success Status: total_raised ≥ target
  • Net Profit: total_raised - total_costs
  • ROI: (net_profit / total_costs) × 100%
  • Total Tokens Sold: Σ D(t) over all days
  • Average Token Price: total_raised / total_tokens

Principal-Agent Framework

EquiCurve models the economic relationship between the platform (principal) and the entrepreneur (agent), addressing information asymmetry and incentive alignment challenges common in crowdfunding.

The Problem

  • Information Asymmetry: Entrepreneurs have private information about project quality that platforms cannot directly observe.
  • Moral Hazard: Entrepreneurs may shirk effort after receiving funding, knowing their effort is not perfectly observable.
  • Adverse Selection: Low-quality projects may mimic high-quality ones, making it hard for platforms to screen effectively.
  • Incentive Misalignment: Platform and entrepreneur objectives may diverge (e.g., platform wants volume, entrepreneur wants success).

The Solution

  • Performance-Based Fees: Platform fees tied to campaign success align incentives and reduce moral hazard.
  • Effort Signaling: Observable effort (marketing, updates) serves as a signal of project quality, helping platforms screen projects.
  • Dynamic Contracts: Adjustable pricing and effort requirements create flexible mechanisms that adapt to revealed information.
  • Threshold Mechanisms: All-or-nothing funding models create commitment devices, ensuring serious entrepreneurs participate.

Parameter Calibration & Estimation

EquiCurve uses log-linear transformation to estimate parameters from historical data, enabling data-driven calibration.

Taking the natural logarithm of both sides:
ln(D(t)) = ln(α) + β × ln(E(t)) - γ × ln(P(t)) + ln(ε(t))
This linear form enables ordinary least squares (OLS) regression

Typical Ranges

  • α: 100 - 5,000 (market size)
  • β: 0.3 - 0.7 (effort elasticity)
  • γ: 0.8 - 1.5 (price elasticity)
  • σ: 0.1 - 0.3 (noise std dev)

Calibration Sources

  • Historical campaign data
  • Industry benchmarks
  • A/B testing results
  • Academic research
  • Platform analytics

Validation

  • R² goodness of fit
  • Out-of-sample testing
  • Benchmark comparison
  • Expert review
  • Sensitivity analysis

Advanced Features & Experiments

Elasticity Analysis

Systematic variation of price elasticity (γ) and effort elasticity (β) to understand how demand sensitivity affects campaign outcomes. Helps identify optimal parameter ranges for different market segments.

Fee Structure Optimization

Comparison of fixed, dynamic, and hybrid fee models. Analyzes how platform fee structures impact entrepreneur incentives and overall campaign success rates.

Duration Optimization

Multi-run simulations across different campaign durations to find the optimal length that maximizes success probability while balancing urgency and awareness-building time.

Market Condition Scenarios

Simulation of bull vs. bear markets, competition effects, and external shocks. Helps entrepreneurs and platforms prepare for various market conditions and adjust strategies accordingly.

Key Insights & Best Practices

For Entrepreneurs

  • • Higher effort (E) consistently increases demand, but with diminishing returns
  • • Dynamic pricing can increase revenue by 15-30% compared to fixed pricing
  • • Front-loading effort is crucial when campaigns are behind schedule
  • • Price elasticity (γ) > 1 means small price changes have large demand effects
  • • Bonding curves work best for projects with strong early adopter communities

For Platforms

  • • Performance-based fees align incentives better than fixed fees
  • • Providing effort visibility helps reduce information asymmetry
  • • Optimal campaign duration varies by project type (typically 14-30 days)
  • • Price elasticity data helps set platform guidelines and recommendations
  • • Multi-round strategies can improve overall success rates
